Video Meeting

Video calls via platforms such as Zoom are becoming ubiquitous in work and educational environments, especially after 2020, as COVID causes people to be forced apart as an alternative to face-to-face meetings.

Video conferencing does restore some essential non-verbal elements: participants can see each other's facial expressions and hear each other's intonation and tone of voice. This makes conveying and interpreting emotions much easier than via email or chat. Expressions become visible and help avoid some misunderstandings. As a result, video calls rank high on the richness scale of communication media because they can convey multiple cues at once.

However, video communication also highlights non-verbal gaps. On the first point, video is limited to what the camera captures, usually the head and shoulders, and the person is confined to a small square. Important nonverbal information, such as full body posture and interpersonal distance is lost. Secondly, due to the position of the camera, it is difficult to achieve direct eye contact. It is not possible for a person's gaze to be focused on the screen and the camera at the same time, so the gaze is always seen to be straying away from the other person, which is contrary to common sense. The camera cannot be positioned in such a way that it is always in focus, and the gaze is staggered.

In group videoconferencing, where people need to focus on a large screen, it's also not easy to see everyone's mini-screens at the same time, so using the rest of the eye doesn't work to a large extent. Additionally, latency can be an issue, with immediacy becoming a random signal that usually doesn't get the message across to the other person in the moment of reaction. For participants in video calls, they have to work harder to read and send nonverbal signals, which creates more fatigue; communicating via video requires a high level of concentration, with the gaze going back and forth all the time while the head is not rotating, which goes against human communication instincts, and the quality of the screen will also accelerate eye fatigue.

Videoconferencing has also been improved, and now tries to use virtual raise your hand button or like icon and other features to simulate prompts without the need for verbal interruptions. Modern platforms allow users to analogize their head movements and gestures, and nods in the virtual, thus restoring a form of non-verbal feedback. This can greatly enhance social presence. These features improve the fluidity of the meeting, and with these design interventions, everything is aimed at making it closer to a real meeting.

Discord

In Discord, people don't just stay in the channel and communicate, or in this case, communication exists as a medium, where friends can watch movies and enjoy music together, or go on adventures in virtual bars or deep sea and high skies in games.

Communication also becomes a medium for friends to watch movies and enjoy music together, or to go exploring in a virtual bar or deep sea skies in a game to increase interaction. However, these avatars are often unable to accurately convey real emotions and are often based on another medium, which can change moods for better or worse.

All of this distances people from each other and makes friends more like teammates than close friends.

Emojis, GIFs, Reaction Buttons, Typing Indicators, Read Receipts

Emojis function as digital facial expressions. GIFs simulate gestures or tone. Typing dots signal anticipation. Read receipts confirm acknowledgment.

These elements attempt to replicate social presence. They provide micro-signals that suggest I am here.

But they remain abstractions.
